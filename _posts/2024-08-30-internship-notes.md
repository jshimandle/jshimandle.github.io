I went in expecting to learn how industry worked. I did. I also learned several things I hadn't anticipated.

## What I Expected

CAD. Manufacturing drawings. Engineering change orders. Process. The gap between
academic design and production-ready design. All of that was there, and it was
genuinely useful.

I expected the pace to be faster than school. It was slower, in ways that surprised me —
not because people weren't working hard, but because decisions at scale require
coordination that classes don't simulate.

## The Coordination Problem

The hardest part of my internship wasn't technical. It was figuring out who needed to
know what, and when. A design change that would take me two hours in SolidWorks
could require a week of conversations — procurement, manufacturing, quality, program
management — before anyone would sign off.

This isn't bureaucratic dysfunction (though that exists too). It's a rational response
to the fact that in a company making thousands of parts, a casual change in one component
can propagate consequences that no single engineer can fully anticipate. The process is
slow because the system is complex and the cost of errors is real.

School doesn't teach this, because school's systems are small enough that coordination
isn't the bottleneck.

## On Being New

I was wrong three times in front of colleagues in ways I couldn't easily recover from.
Once about a material property I was confident I remembered correctly (I was off by
a factor of two). Once about a manufacturing constraint I hadn't known to ask about.
Once because I answered a question I hadn't fully understood.

Each of these felt significant at the time. Looking back, they were small, correctable,
and — I think — roughly what being new to something actually looks like.

The engineers I respected most weren't the ones who were never wrong. They were the ones
who identified their errors quickly, communicated them clearly, and fixed them without
drama.

That seems like a practice worth developing deliberately, before the stakes get higher.

## What I'm Still Thinking About

Whether I want to work in a large organization long-term. The resources are real —
equipment, mentors, scope of work. The constraints are also real. I'm not sure the
answer is no, but I'm not sure it's yes.

How to keep making things with my hands as engineering work becomes more computational.
The shop floor and the simulation suite feel like they belong together. I want to keep
them that way.

What the engineers who seemed most satisfied had in common — and whether that's something
you choose or something you become.

I'll write more when I know more.
