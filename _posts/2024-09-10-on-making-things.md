The first time I cut a thread on a manual lathe, I made three unusable parts before the fourth one fit. The machine shop instructor — a man of few words and strong opinions about coolant — watched all four attempts without comment. When the thread finally engaged cleanly, he nodded once and walked away.

That ratio has stayed with me.

## What Simulation Misses

Engineering curricula have gotten very good at teaching analysis. I can model stress distributions in a bracket I've never touched. I can simulate fluid flow through a valve I couldn't identify by sight. The software is genuinely remarkable and I use it constantly.

But simulation has a rounding error that compounds: it allows you to skip the part where things go wrong in ways you didn't predict.

In a FEA model, material is homogeneous and boundary conditions are clean. In a machine shop, the bar stock has an inclusion you didn't know about, the workholding slips slightly, and the insert you chose was the wrong grade for the material. The simulation never told you about any of this — not because it was wrong, but because it modeled a world where such things don't happen.

## The Discipline of Irreversibility

Digital work has an undo button. Physical work does not.

Once you've parted a workpiece too short, you can't add material back. Once you've over-torqued a fastener and stripped the threads, the part is compromised. The consequences of inattention are immediate and permanent in a way that a crashed CAD file — annoying as it is — simply isn't.

This irreversibility is not an inconvenience. It is the entire point.

Working within constraints that cannot be negotiated away produces a different quality of attention. You slow down at the right moments. You measure twice. You think about what could go wrong before it does, rather than after.

## The Epistemology of Touch

There's knowledge that lives in the hands and doesn't transfer to text. The sound a drill makes when it's about to break. The feel of a file when it's cutting versus skating across hardened steel. The moment, in hand sewing, when the needle finds the right path through the material.

None of this is mystical. It's pattern recognition developed through repetition. But it is *embodied* knowledge — it resides in the nervous system, not the prefrontal cortex — and it cannot be downloaded. It has to be accumulated through iteration.

This is, I think, what the machine shop teaches that no lecture can: that some knowledge requires you to show up and be wrong several times before you are right.

---

The thread I cut on that fourth attempt is still on the workpiece, sitting on a shelf in my apartment. The fit isn't perfect — there's maybe 15% more play than the design called for — but it works. I keep it as a reminder that competence is mostly just the accumulated residue of not-quite-right.
