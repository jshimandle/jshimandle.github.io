*A prose poem I wrote for my poetry workshop class, spring of 2026. About life and growing up.*

---
A car is revving its engine outside my bedroom window, and asking what it means to be a man. It wants to be big and strong and know what it means—and how it works—and who built it.

I built it—and it works for me—the meaning is my own. I scraped it and scrapped it and baked it and backed it out of the drive way I was born in. People brought up in beautiful places don’t see beauty in the dirt, so I’m glad that I was raised in just a plain white bungalow. Are we beautiful? Not ever in quite the way we think, and always in some ways that we will never know. Maybe it’s a good question to ask because it cannot be answered. Maybe the purpose of life is to wonder. 

It built me—it worked me—it gave me license for meaning. It scraped me and scoured me and broke my body. And I bled out on back alley asphalt, just like the boy from three doors down who tore his leg down to the bone on our chain link fence in a pick-up game back in 2012. Did you know chameleons burst into a thousand different colors the moment before they die? We die a million little times each minute and call it growth or change or progress. What is life but the witnessing of death—this world’s and our own? Memory stored in little mason jars, slid from shelves as the earth quaked. And we are left to sweep up the glass of that little life lived only gently in the lighter passage of sharp softening time—hope carried to the bin like a newborn child.

She built it—she cared for it and cried—and loved me more than lovers ever could.  Plain white bungalows look just fine, unless they’re on the canal in Copenhagen. Cheerleaders are only appreciated when they’re gone, and so sons call home when they find: the world will not love them like a mother. Life is beautiful. What then is death but beauty complete? The weight of experience and emotion wrapped up together, all one and the same—a beautiful brown in woven rainbow.
